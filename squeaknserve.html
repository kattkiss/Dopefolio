Squeak N' Serve (2025) (PC)

A third person platforming/cooking game about a rat and his food truck in a world dominated by cats.

Engine/Tools: Unity, Yarnspinner & Blender
Role(s): Game Designer, 3D & VFX Artist
Team Size: 4
Duration: Mar 2025 - Jun 2025
Type: University group project

Download the game here

Description of project

You are a rat. A rat with a food truck (that you inherited when your uncle went to prison) and a love for food, but the world is ruled by cats who look down on rats. Can you keep your secrecy and serve amazing food according to the customers wishes and prove that rats can indeed be cooks? Or will you serve shit food and get caught by the kitty cops as well?

This game was made by me and some friends as part of our last "big" game project of our bachelor degrees. I was one of two (2) designers in this project, thus we ended up splitting the design work. I was going to focus on designing the games systems, with close communication with our programmer, while the other designer took care of level design.

Design Goal

When the idea of a cooking game got pitched to me, I was analyzing some existing cooking games and saw that they had a trend in almost always being about using time to pressure the player. What if a cooking game didn't do this? Here is my design thinking:

The Problem
Cooking games too often use time to challenge the player.

The Objective
Create a cooking game that uses other elements than time to create a sense of challenge.

The Approach
- Through system design, challenge the player in ways that are not primarily time-limited.
- The player should feel pressured to make a perfect dish rather than a quick one.

System: Ordering

Why this mechanic exists
- To simulate the experience of working in a restaurant (Food truck).
- To make the player navigate the environment.
- To make the player critically think about what dish they should be cooking.

How it works
1. The player picks up the chefs hat and walks up to the order window.
2. The player presses a button to take the order.
3. The customer will vaguely tell the player what they want to eat. For example: "I want something with tomatoes".
4. An order has now been taken.

Expected outcome
- Encourage the player to be attentive to what the customer is saying.
- The player makes educated decisions based on what dish they think would fit each customer best.

System: Cooking

Why this mechanic exists
- To simulate the experience of working in a restaurant (Food truck).
- To give the player a reason to navigate the environment.
- The main way for the player to progress.

How it works
- Split into three (3) stations: Boiling, Cutting & Frying.
- Boiling: Player throws an ingredient into the station and presses a button to begin boiling.
- Cutting: Player throws an ingredient into the station and repeatedly presses a button to cut it up.
    - (Design-phase only) Minigame: The player needs to time their button presses to when the indicator hits the sweet spot. See an example of this below.

- Frying: Mechanically the same as Boiling.
- Mixes can be made by throwing an ingredient on top of another at a station.
- Preset dishes can be made by following the recipes specified in the recipe book.

Expected outcome
- The player cooks the dish according to its recipe in the recipe book.
- The player gets mechanical experience of cooking food.

System: Recipes

Why this mechanic exists
- To ease decisionmaking by the player.
- To be used for Grading.

How it works
- Each available recipe can be found in the recipe book.
- A recipe consists of: A base ingredient, one or more sides and cooking instructions.

Expected outcome
- The player has a starting point for deciding what dish to cook.
- Amount of dishes & sides can be increased/decreased to determine the level of difficulty & complexity for the player, such as when progressing through the game.

System: Grading

Why this mechanic exists
To reflect the player's performance in the game.

How it works
- The player picks up the dish and throws it back to the customer.
- 4 levels of grading: Trash, Ok, Good & Perfect.
    - Trash: The player didn't use the correct ingredients/didn't cook them correctly.
    - Ok: The player used and cooked the correct base.
    - Good: The player used and cooked the correct base & sides.
    - (Design-phase only) Perfect: The player used and cooked the correct base, sides & Seasoning.
- No grade leads to a Game Over.

Expected outcome
- To not discourage the player from making mistakes as a result of experimentation but...
- ...Reward the player for performing well and in turn...
- ...Encourage the player to make perfect dishes.

Previous iterations
This system was before about each category of a dish increasing/reducing a final score based on the player's performance. On reflection, this was interpreted to be overengineered and thus thrown away and replace by the system above. Example of the old system can be found below.

(Design-phase only) System: Food temperature

Why this mechanic exists
- To add complexity and difficulty to making perfect dishes.
- To reduce perceived downtime during gameplay.

How it works
- Boiling and Frying heats up ingredients.
- If the ingredient isn't being cooked, the heat level will decrease over time.
- The heat level is used in grading of dishes. For example: The recipe requires the food to be warm. It needs to have a specific heat level when turned in to be graded as Perfect.

Expected outcome
Players put more thought into the order they cook dishes to minimize heat loss before handing it in.

(Design-phase only) System: Seasoning

Why this mechanic exists
- To add complexity and difficulty to the cooking process.
- To add more variation to dishes.

How it works
- Each dish has 3 categories of taste: Sweetness, Heat, Saltiness.
- Each taste category is on a scale of 1 - 5.
- Individual ingredients and the final dish can be seasoned.
- The player can taste the food to see what value the taste categories are on.
    - Can be done up to two (2) times without ruining the dish.
- What each category should sit on is determined by the customer and the recipe.

Expected outcome
- Player needs to be precise in their seasoning.
- Player learns how to get to the seasoning "sweet-spot" from experience, like a true chef!

Tutorial

Why this exists
- To onboard players to the game's cooking mechanics because...
- ...Playtesters felt lost, unsure of what they were supposed to do.

The Approach
- Player learns by doing, not by reading.
- Used icons (such as big arrows) instead of words, when possible.
- Game tells the player what to do, step-by-step.
- Big arrow above player points to where they need to go at all times.

Art & Presentation

- An "ugly", grimy and blocky artstyle was used to support the game's atmosphere and the skills of the team.
- Made and used smoke VFX to communicate vital information to the player. For example: When something is cooking, when they are sprinting, etc.
- Modeled most of the dishes and their ingredients.

Playtesting & Iteration

What was tested
- Does the artstyle fit the game?
- Is the gameplay-loop fun?
- The quality of the tutorial (Onboarding process).

Feedback summary
- A dirty and raw art style fits a rat in a food truck.
- Feedback about the tutorial:
    - Handholding: Mixed response, too much for some and just right for others.
    - Too much text?: In general, no. Good that essential words are highlighted.
    - Anything left unclear? No.
- The gameplay-loop was generally appreciated but there could be...
    - Less downtime.
    - More challenge.
    - More variation.

Changes made
- Designs made for minigames when cooking dishes.
- Designs made for new gameplay-elements , food temperature and Seasoning.

Summary of outcome

- Playtests revealed areas for improvement in specific mechanics of the gameplay-loop.
- Game is believed to be a tad too easy.
- Solid onboarding process.

Lessons learned

Design
- Got to practice designing individual, interconnected systems.
- Designed my first tutorial in a game.

3D Art & VFX
- Learned how to use and make my own VFX systems in Unity.
- Practiced 3D modeling in accordance to an established art style with a team consisting of more than one artist.
