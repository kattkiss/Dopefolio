The Shoe (2026)

Short Visual Novel-hybrid about drunk parties and having one of your shoes stolen.

Live Link

Project Overview

The Shoe is about you, the player, waking up in the middle of the night at a home party. When you decide to put on your shoes and go home, something isn't right. You can only find one of your limited edition Matsune Hiku shoes. Where did the other one go? You obviously can't walk in the cold winter snow with a bare foot, can you?

This game was quite a passion project that me and my significant other made, me being the Game Designer/Artist/Programmer and them being the Writer/Narrative Designer. The initial idea was that the story would be something silly and the game be something simple and fun. While the idea remained in our heads for a while, we didn't find the time to actually begin working on it until the summer of 2025. We did take a break in September of that year but resumed development by January 2026.

Game Design

While I can appreciate visual novels to a certain point, I feel that they often turn into just that, a visual novel...not a GAME.

From my own experience, I usually end up just reading text on the screen, accompanied by some artwork in the background. After a while, I will end up skimming through the text until something interesting happens, as they are often linear, not leaving many meaningful choices or a sense of agency to the player.

THIS is what I wanted to change with this game. It was still going to contain what you would expect from a visual novel, but also contain choices that affect the player's experience and require them to act quickly during important moments of the story, making even the "skimmers" pay attention.

Time-Sensitive Choices

The first mechanic I added to the formula was Time-Sensitive Choices. The player would have a pop-up of dialogue choices they can make, and they would be on a timer while making their choice. Not making a choice in time would have the game make it for them, taking away their agency.

The idea behind this is that I am taking away the player's ability to tackle the game at their own pace, essentially challenging them. Without a mechanic like this, one might be like me and get tired of reading after a while. Having this mechanic in the game, I believe that it adds some tension/friction which could help with keeping players engaged for longer than they otherwise would.

Another good thing about adding a mechanic like this to the game is that if the player wants to have a desirable result from the interaction, they would need to have understood what they had read before to make an educated decision. This does also, in turn, put the player's focus on the story which is the main selling point of a game in this genre.

This mechanic was implemented in times it felt the most natural, such as in conversations (Don't let there be too much dead silence!) and when the player had to react quickly (someone is about to vomit on you).

Quick Time Events

By incorporating Time-Sensitive Choices, I'm challenging the player's attention to detail. With Quick Time Events I'm challenging their motor skills.

To essentially break up the flow of the game and make it less repetitive, not just being a few Time-Sensitive Choices, I needed to add something else. It needed to be something that challenged a different skill from the player so that it wouldn't feel too same-y. I thought a kind of Quick Time Event would do nicely.

The concept is simple, the player has a cursor that is moving from side to side, and they need to time their button presses to when the cursor is within the "sweet spot". The speed of the cursor increases and the width of the "sweet spot" decreases with each successful buttonpress.

I decided to use this mechanic specifically for when the player in the game is performing something that requires proper motor skills, such as ascending a set of stairs while intoxicated. This way it feels natural for the player to be performing the task when put into the context of the scenario in the game.

Levels Of Intoxication

As The Shoe is a game about experiences when under the influence, I thought that the player should also feel like they are. Therefore, I added a shader that made the background move and would intensify/detensify depending on whether the player decided to sober up or continue drinking.

Art

All the artwork used in the game are stock photos that I heavily edited in Adobe Photoshop to give them distorted faces (caused by the player being intoxicated or face-blind) and a cartoon-like appearance.
